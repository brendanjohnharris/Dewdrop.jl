# Benchmarks

Dewdrop is benchmarked against the established simulators (Brian2 and brian2cuda, BrainPy,
NEST and NEST GPU, and GeNN) on the **same** network: a recurrent excitatory/inhibitory
[`AdEx`](@ref) net with a fixed in-degree and current-based exponential synapses, the regime where
the sparse synaptic scatter dominates. Every simulator builds an *identical* connectome (a
language-agnostic splitmix64 sample, verified edge-for-edge) and is checked to produce statistically
matching firing rates and CV-ISI before any timing is compared, so the numbers are apples-to-apples.

## Headline numbers

Wall-clock time (lower is better) and peak memory at a mid-size and a large network. The relative
ordering is the point; absolute values depend on the machine.

**CPU**

| Simulator (CPU) | time @ N=16k | time @ N=512k | memory @ N=512k |
|---|--:|--:|--:|
| Dewdrop [`Fused`](@ref) | 0.36 s | **7.5 s** | 3.8 GB |
| Dewdrop [`Turbo`](@ref) | 0.54 s | 20.8 s | 3.9 GB |
| Dewdrop [`Serial`](@ref) | 1.97 s | 56.3 s | 3.8 GB |
| Brian2 (C++ standalone) | 0.38 s | 25.6 s | 3.3 GB |
| BrainPy (JAX) | 1.33 s | 25.4 s | 4.2 GB |
| NEST | 1.83 s | 116.7 s | 9.5 GB |

**GPU**

| Simulator (GPU) | time @ N=16k | time @ N=512k | memory @ N=512k |
|---|--:|--:|--:|
| Dewdrop [`Fused`](@ref) | 0.069 s | **0.24 s** | 1.3 GB |
| GeNN (CUDA) | 0.067 s | 1.94 s | 3.6 GB |
| brian2cuda | 0.145 s | 0.53 s | 2.7 GB |
| NEST GPU | 0.32 s | 1.40 s | 1.7 GB |

## What the scaling shows

- **CPU, at scale.** Dewdrop's [`Fused`](@ref) backend is the fastest CPU simulator measured: at
  N=512k it is **3.4× faster than Brian2's compiled C++**, 3.4× faster than BrainPy, and 15.6× faster
  than NEST, with no code-generation or compile step.
- **CPU, small networks.** [`Fused`](@ref) (0.36 s) sits within a few percent of Brian2's hand-tuned
  C++ standalone (0.38 s) at N=16k: a pure-Julia engine at near-compiled-C++ throughput.
- **GPU, small networks.** Dewdrop's GPU megakernel ties GeNN's generated CUDA (0.069 vs 0.067 s) and
  is roughly 2× faster than brian2cuda and 4-5× faster than NEST GPU: the launch-bound regime where
  the fused step removes per-phase kernel launches.
- **GPU, at scale.** Dewdrop is fastest by a clear margin: at N=512k it is 2.2× faster than
  brian2cuda, 5.9× faster than NEST GPU, and 8× faster than GeNN, whose generated kernels degrade
  superlinearly there. It is also the leanest on the device at scale (1.3 GB).
- **Memory.** On the CPU, Dewdrop is mid-pack (Brian2's standalone is the leanest, NEST by far the
  heaviest); on the GPU at scale, Dewdrop uses the least memory of the four.

The combination (competitive at small sizes on both devices and the fastest at scale) comes from
the struct-of-arrays state, the fused single-pass step that pipelines on one device stream, and the
compacted spike scatter that keeps the GPU off the memory-bandwidth wall (see [running on the
GPU](guide/gpu.md)).

## Methodology

The shared problem (one `spec.toml` consumed by every simulator):

- **Network.** Recurrent E/I [`AdEx`](@ref), 80% excitatory, fixed in-degree K=100, CUBA exponential
  synapses with a constant external drive: the sparse-scatter-dominated regime.
- **Connectome.** Generated by a language-agnostic splitmix64 algorithm so every simulator builds the
  identical graph; the comparison scripts verify the edge count matches across all simulators.
- **Run.** `dt = 0.1` ms, 500 ms for the throughput scan (one network size per octave from 2k to
  512k), 1000 ms for the correctness run that checks firing rate and CV-ISI agree.
- **Measured.** End-to-end wall time of the integration and peak resident memory, per simulator, per
  size, on CPU and (where supported) GPU. Absolute timings are machine-dependent; the scaling slopes
  and the relative ordering are the reproducible result.

Each simulator is implemented as its own self-contained runner under
`test/simulator_comparisons/<sim>/` (Dewdrop in Julia, Brian2/BrainPy/NEST/GeNN in Python), writing
standard `out/performance.csv` files; `compare_simulators.jl` discovers them, verifies they ran the
same simulation, and produces the figure above. Adding a simulator is dropping in a directory.

## Reproducing

From the package's `test/simulator_comparisons/` directory, run each simulator's runner and then the
aggregator (see that directory's `README.md` and `run_all.sh`):

```bash
julia --project=test dewdrop/run.jl        # Dewdrop, all sizes, CPU + GPU
python brian/run.py                        # each other simulator in its own environment
julia --project=test compare_simulators.jl # verify + plot to out/comparison.pdf
```
